# **Catalog and Analysis of Archival Data Sources for Internal Combustion Engine Transient Dynamics and Driven-Dissipative Settling Behaviors**

The internal combustion engine (ICE) presents one of the most historically significant and empirically rich examples of a highly nonlinear, driven-dissipative system. Within the framework of non-equilibrium thermodynamics and advanced control theory, the ICE operates by balancing a "driven" component—the intermittent, discrete release of chemical energy via combustion—against multiple "dissipative" components, including internal mechanical friction, viscous fluid drag, pumping losses, accessory loads, and thermodynamic irreversibilities.1 The dynamic equilibrium between these opposing forces is most critically observed and challenged during transient maneuvers. Specifically, the return-to-idle sequence—often referred to as a tip-out, throttle blip, or rev-drop transient—and the subsequent sustained idle state provide the most rigorous testing ground for understanding the fundamental physics of the system.

For researchers sourcing high-fidelity, time-series data of engine revolutions per minute (![][image1]) to analyze these transient and settling behaviors, the historical window between 1975 and 2000 represents an unparalleled "sweet spot." This era encapsulates the monumental automotive engineering transition from purely mechanical and pneumatic carburetion to highly sophisticated, microprocessor-based Electronic Engine Control (EEC) systems. During this quarter-century, academic institutions, major automotive manufacturers, and control-engineering researchers generated exhaustive empirical datasets to validate the first generation of digital, nonlinear engine models. This report provides a comprehensive, expert-level catalog of pointers to archival sources, university repositories, dataset names, and control-engineering journal citations that contain high-resolution transient data. Furthermore, this analysis will deeply investigate the mathematical nature of the return-to-idle settling tail, explicitly addressing the debate over whether the asymptotic decay of the engine speed is purely exponential or exhibits slower-than-exponential, heavy-tailed dynamics at long times.

## **Thermodynamic and Kinematic Framework of the Driven-Dissipative Engine**

To accurately locate, interpret, and utilize the correct archival datasets, it is necessary to establish the theoretical and thermodynamic parameters that governed data collection during the 1975–2000 period. The classical formulations of thermodynamics, pioneered by Carnot and Clausius, defined the entropy and efficiency of reversible heat engines operating between thermal reservoirs.1 However, the modern formulation of the internal combustion engine requires extending these principles to include irreversible processes and non-equilibrium dynamics.

In a driven-dissipative framework, the engine is modeled as an oscillator where the continuous dissipation of energy is counteracted by periodic driving impulses. Researchers such as Watowich, Nitzan, Ross, Mozurkewich, and Berry extensively analyzed light-driven dissipative engines, determining piston paths that provide maximum work output and minimum entropy generation using optimal control theory.4 When these principles are applied to the physical ICE, the system at idle functions fundamentally as a limit-cycle oscillator operating constantly at the boundary of stability.6

The idle speed control (ISC) problem is inherently nonlinear, time-varying, and plagued by system uncertainties. A defining characteristic of this system is the induction-to-power transport delay.6 Air entering the throttle body must traverse the intake manifold, enter the combustion cylinder, undergo compression, and ignite before any torque is generated to counteract the ongoing dissipation. This transport delay is inversely proportional to the engine speed. Consequently, as the engine rapidly decelerates during a tip-out maneuver, the transport delay exponentially increases, pushing the closed-loop feedback system toward instability.6

If the feedback gains of the engine control unit (ECU) are not perfectly calibrated to match the nonlinear dissipative forces at that exact moment, the system enters a stable limit cycle. This limit cycle is physically observable as a low-frequency oscillation or "hunting" in the sustained idle RPM data.6 Archival data from this era frequently captures these sub-harmonic vibrations and stick-slip limit cycles, providing a rich empirical basis for modeling engines as driven-dissipative oscillators.7 Researchers investigating the bifurcation structures of these systems will find that the interaction between the global gain (fuel injection) and dissipation (mechanical friction) mirrors other driven-dissipative phenomena across physics, such as the mode-locked states of lasers and the autowave dynamics observed in rotating detonation engines.3 The frequency of these limit-cycle oscillations is generally defined as the reciprocal of the transport delay period, meaning that the shorter the transport delay (at higher RPMs), the higher the frequency of the limit cycle.6

## **The Tip-Out Transient: Physical Mechanics and Data Signatures**

The most valuable data for analyzing driven-dissipative settling behavior is found in tip-out or throttle-release transients. During a tip-in (acceleration), the system is heavily driven; during a tip-out, the driving force is abruptly curtailed, isolating the dissipative mechanisms of the rotating assembly. Analyzing the ![][image1] time-series data during this specific phase requires an understanding of the cascading physical events that occur when the throttle plate slams shut.

Upon throttle closure, the manifold absolute pressure (MAP) crashes, creating a high vacuum state. The air mass flow rate drops near-instantaneously, but the fuel delivery lags significantly. In carbureted and port-fuel-injected (PFI) engines, a film of liquid fuel constantly lines the walls of the intake manifold and cylinder ports. During a high-vacuum tip-out, this fuel puddle rapidly evaporates and is drawn into the cylinder, creating a momentary but severe rich spike in the air-fuel ratio (AFR).10 This phenomenon, known as wall-wetting dynamics, means the engine continues to generate unexpected, un-commanded torque for several cycles after the throttle is closed, resisting the natural mechanical deceleration of the rotating mass.

Furthermore, the sudden drop in engine speed alters the hydrodynamic friction in the engine's journal bearings. The oil film thickness changes, shifting the boundary friction coefficients. Simultaneously, the pumping losses increase as the pistons fight against the high manifold vacuum.13 The convolution of these rapidly shifting forces—aerodynamic pumping, hydrodynamic friction, and lagging combustion torque—creates a highly complex transient response curve. Archival datasets that capture these moments at high sampling rates are critical for identifying the true mathematical nature of the engine's decay profile.

## **Asymptotic Decay: Exponential vs. Slower-than-Exponential Tails**

A primary theoretical inquiry regarding the rev-and-drop transient is whether the final approach to the idle setpoint—the "settling tail"—exhibits an exponential decay or a slower-than-exponential (e.g., power-law or fractional) decay at long times.

Classical linear models of engine dynamics approximate the rotational inertia and frictional dissipation of the engine utilizing a basic Voigt element. In this rheological model, the system is represented by a mass supported by a linear spring (representing elastic stiffness) and a linear viscous dashpot (representing the dissipative viscous response).14 In a strictly linear, time-invariant (LTI) driven-dissipative model, the cessation of the driving force results in a standard exponential decay following the formula ![][image2], where ![][image3] is the system's fundamental time constant.16 If this classical model holds true, plotting the natural logarithm of the ![][image1] data against time during the settling tail should yield a straight line.

However, a rigorous review of empirical data from the 1975–2000 transition era strongly suggests that the true settling tail of an internal combustion engine is slower-than-exponential. The assumption of an exponential decay relies on the premise of a memoryless, Markovian dissipation process. In reality, the engine's deceleration is governed by the superposition of multiple interacting dynamic states, each operating on highly disparate time scales:

1. **Manifold Filling and Emptying Dynamics:** The rapid equalization of pressure in the intake manifold operates on a fast time scale, typically measured in tens of milliseconds.22  
2. **Fuel Wall-Wetting and Evaporation:** The accumulation and subsequent evaporation of the fuel puddle in the intake port operates on a medium time scale, often taking several engine cycles or hundreds of milliseconds to fully resolve.10  
3. **Thermal and Frictional Shifts:** The gradual change in oil viscosity, cylinder wall temperature, and block temperature during deceleration operates on a slow time scale, often extending across seconds or even minutes.23  
4. **ECU Filtering and Sensor Lags:** Digital filters utilized in early engine control units typically employed an exponentially weighted moving average (e.g., a 90/10 filter where 90% of the old data is averaged with 10% of the new data). This algorithmic intervention artificially elongated the tail of the response.26

When these multiple exponential relaxation rates are superimposed, the resulting uninterrupted decay exhibits a heavy-tailed, slower-than-exponential trajectory. In mathematical terms, this long-time asymptotic behavior is often modeled using Mittag-Leffler functions or piecewise power laws to account for the long-memory effects inherent in the system's multiphysics dissipation.20 High-resolution oscilloscope data of step-responses in automotive analog control circuits from this era explicitly note the presence of an elongated "settling tail" that deviates from simple exponential assumptions, often causing a long-time delay before the system finally reaches absolute steady-state.27 The superposition of a faster-than-exponential component with a slower-than-exponential component generally results in the slower term dominating the long-time asymptotic behavior.20

## **The Dashpot Effect: Mechanical vs. Electronic Interventions**

When sourcing and analyzing archival ![][image1] data, researchers must rigorously distinguish between the engine's natural physical dissipation and artificial interventions designed to alter the deceleration curve. The most prominent of these interventions is the "dashpot" function.

In the late carburetor and early fuel-injection eras, a physical pneumatic dashpot was placed in series with the throttle linkage to mechanically retard the throttle plate's return to the idle stop.32 The purpose of this device was to prevent the engine from stalling due to a sudden lack of airflow and to reduce the massive spike in unburned hydrocarbon emissions that occurs under high deceleration vacuum. Time-series RPM data from engines equipped with mechanical dashpots typically display a forced linear or simple exponential decay, as the mechanical damping of the pneumatic dashpot physically dominates the engine's natural rotational inertia and internal friction.33

With the advent of electronic Idle Air Control (IAC) valves and drive-by-wire motorized throttle bodies (MTB), the physical pneumatic dashpot was replaced by an electronic dashpot algorithm coded into the ECU's read-only memory.12 During a deceleration transient, the ECU commands the IAC bypass valve or the electronic throttle to remain partially open, gradually decrementing its position as the engine speed drops.36 Datasets capturing this era reveal an explicit separation between the engine's natural dissipative decay and the controller's artificial softening of the landing.

Variables to search for in these historical tuning datasets include the "dashpot decrement rate," the "IAC offset," and the "dashpot time constant".37 Modifying these parameters fundamentally changes the mathematical shape of the settling tail. If the dashpot time constant is programmed to be too long, the RPM data exhibits a "hanging" rev, failing to return to idle promptly. Conversely, if the decrement rate is too aggressive, the RPM drops rapidly past the idle setpoint, triggering a proportional-integral-derivative (PID) recovery response. This recovery often results in an underdamped oscillation—a dip and surge—before the limit cycle settles.36 Archival tuning data and modern digital decoders specifically note that the speed change is purposefully programmed to be exponential as it approaches the target, simulating a soft landing into the idle setpoint to maximize passenger comfort and driveline stability.36 Therefore, distinguishing between a naturally slower-than-exponential physical settling tail and an algorithmically imposed exponential dashpot decay is the paramount challenge for researchers utilizing this historical data.

## **University Research Repositories and Institutional Archives**

To acquire raw dataset matrices, unprocessed MATLAB/Simulink models, and un-truncated time-series telemetry, university thesis archives are invaluable. The engine laboratories at Stanford University, the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT), the University of Michigan, The Ohio State University, and Linköping University were the primary hubs for transient engine control research during the target 1975–2000 era. The following sections detail the specific methodologies, principal investigators, and archival signatures of these institutions.

### **Stanford University: Event-Based Observation and In-Cylinder Pressure Control**

The Guidance and Control Laboratory and the Mechanical Engineering department at Stanford University, heavily guided by Professor J. David Powell, generated some of the most exact and mathematically rigorous transient engine data of the late 20th century. Stanford's archives are the primary source for researchers seeking data free from the aliasing effects of traditional time-based sampling.

A critical methodological shift pioneered at Stanford was the transition from time-synchronous to event-synchronous (crank-angle domain) sampling. Traditional automotive ECUs and laboratory data loggers logged parameters at a fixed temporal frequency (e.g., 50 Hz or 100 Hz). However, an internal combustion engine is an intrinsically discrete event generator; combustions happen at specific crank angles, not at specific times. During a rapid deceleration transient, the time between combustion events increases non-linearly. Therefore, time-based sampling heavily aliases the underlying discrete dynamics.

To solve this, Powell and Nicholas P. Fekete (1995) utilized a single-cylinder Cooperative Fuel Research (CFR) engine equipped with a drive-by-wire throttle instrumented to synchronize perfectly with the intake stroke.42 By propagating the nonlinear engine model as an embedded "event-based observer," they captured cycle-by-cycle air-fuel ratio and ![][image1] decay data during throttle tip-out transients.42 For researchers analyzing the true mathematical nature of the dissipative settling tail, Stanford's archival theses—such as Mont Hubbard's 1975 dissertation on the automatic control of IC engines 43 and Fekete's 1989/1995 work on observer-based control 42—contain true crank-angle resolution data. This high-fidelity data eliminates the artificial smoothing of time-based logging, allowing for a highly precise mathematical fit to determine if the purely physical decay is exponential or power-law based.

Furthermore, Powell's pioneering work on utilizing in-cylinder piezoelectric pressure transducers provides direct, real-time feedback of the indicated combustion torque (the driving force).44 Accessing datasets that include this cylinder pressure history clearly delineates the exact millisecond the driving force ceases and the system transitions to pure mechanical dissipation, providing the perfect ![][image4] starting point for analyzing the settling tail.44

### **Ford Motor Company and University of Michigan: The Mean Value Engine Model**

The development of the Mean Value Engine Model (MVEM) is central to historical engine control data. Researchers B.K. Powell and J.A. Cook at the Ford Scientific Research Laboratory collaborated extensively with academic researchers, notably J.W. Grizzle at the University of Illinois and later the University of Michigan, to model the inherently multi-rate sampling dynamics of fuel-injected engines.22

Their groundbreaking research treated the idling engine as a hybrid continuous-discrete sampled data system. The datasets generated from these studies (circa 1987–1990) involve six-cylinder, electronically fuel-injected spark ignition engines where the microprocessor fueling logic operated at a fixed temporal frequency, while the physical breathing, compression, and combustion events operated at a variable frequency dependent entirely on the instantaneous engine speed.22 The complex interaction and phase-shifting between these two domains create highly complex, slower-than-exponential settling dynamics. Pointers to this data can be found in the 1987 American Control Conference proceedings and the 1988 IEEE Control Systems Magazine publications by Cook and Powell, which feature sample transient response validation plots mapping changes in throttle angle, spark timing, delayed fuel, and disturbance torque directly to engine speed.55

Following this era, the Automotive Research Center (ARC) at the University of Michigan, directed by prominent researchers like Anna Stefanopoulou (who transitioned from Ford Motor Company in the late 1990s) and Zoran Filipi, became a massive repository for transient engine data.46 While much of the early MVEM work focused on spark-ignition, ARC's archives contain highly relevant empirical data on transient diesel operations during aggressive tip-in and tip-out maneuvers. Hagena, Filipi, and Assanis (2006) note that transient operations produce significantly different emissions and torque profiles than steady-state maps.46 A tip-out maneuver creates a sudden thermodynamic imbalance; the air supply decays rapidly while the fuel film on the port walls continues to evaporate, leading to momentary rich spikes.11 ARC's repositories contain continuous time-series telemetry capturing these exact transient imbalances, providing a clear view of the dissipative forces at play.

### **The Ohio State University: Center for Automotive Research (CAR)**

The Ohio State University's Center for Automotive Research (OSU-CAR) generated extensive driving cycle and transient data, particularly relating to idle stability and the early transition to hybrid-electric architectures. Research by S. Yurkovich explicitly addressed the "induction-to-power delay" using sliding mode control and combined controller-observer designs specifically formulated for time-delay systems.7

OSU-CAR archives contain high-resolution empirical data on the active compensation of engine speed fluctuations at sustained idle. Because the engine load at idle can change abruptly—due to the engagement of the air conditioning compressor, power steering pump, or alternator—the stabilizing controllers must rapidly manage the nonlinear mechanics of the system.61 Researchers looking for data on camless engine operation, variable valve timing (VVT), and the precise manipulation of volumetric efficiency during deceleration will find Ohio State's mechanical engineering theses highly relevant.23 Furthermore, long-term datasets comprising over 70,000 miles of operation, segmented into over 530 key-on to key-off sessions, offer macroscopic, statistical views of idle settling behavior across thousands of distinct real-world rev-drop events.48

### **MIT and Linköping University: Residual Gas and Compressor Surge Transients**

At the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT), Professor John Heywood's laboratory established the fundamental internal combustion thermodynamic models that defined the era.49 His research group provided critical empirical data on how residual gas fractions—the exhaust gas remaining in the cylinder from the previous combustion cycle—impact idle stability and cyclic variations.49 Because residual gas acts as an inert thermal diluent, it fundamentally alters the specific heat capacity and the heat release rate of the subsequent combustion event. This directly influences the stability of the limit cycle at idle. MIT's archival data interpreting experimental pressure traces through chemical energy release analysis requires acknowledging this residual gas mass fraction.49 This lingering thermal memory effect is a prime physical driver of the slower-than-exponential, long-memory settling tails observed in empirical data.

Linköping University in Sweden, specifically the Vehicular Systems group led by Lars Eriksson and Lars Nielsen, is a premier European source for transient engine control data.52 Their archives, dating from the late 1990s onward, contain exhaustive datasets on aggressive transient maneuvers, including specific tip-outs from targeted RPMs. For example, surge indicator datasets capture the transition from 1500 RPM down to idle, detailing the root-mean-square (RMS) drop in structural vibrations and compressor behavior as the load is suddenly removed.25 The data specifically notes that the tip-out from 1500 RPM is characterized by the largest drop in RMS value before stabilizing at the new load point after approximately 2.5 seconds.25 Linköping's open-access thesis archives (diva-portal.org and ep.liu.se) hold raw validation data for extended mean value engine models, comparing simulated differential equations directly against physical test-bench measurements during rapid deceleration and shutdown events.52

## **Primary Archival Literature: SAE Mobilus and Control Journals (1975–2000)**

The Society of Automotive Engineers (SAE) database (SAE Mobilus) and foundational control engineering journals (such as the IEEE Transactions on Control Systems Technology and IFAC Proceedings) are the premier repositories for graphical and tabulated ![][image1] time-series data. Sourcing from these specific citations will yield the raw step-response plots necessary to digitize and mathematically fit the return-to-idle asymptotic curves.

### **Carburetor Transient Dynamics: The Unfiltered Baseline**

For baseline empirical data on pure, un-filtered engine dissipation prior to the algorithmic intervention of digital ECUs, researchers must look to late-era carburetor studies.

**SAE 770046 (Tanaka & Durbin, 1977): *Transient Response of a Carburetor Engine*.** This paper is a cornerstone for early transient modeling. It provides experimental time-series data on engine response to abrupt throttle motion. The tests were conducted from a steady-state baseline of 1300 RPM, with a manifold depression of \-65.3 kPa.65 The data successfully isolates the dynamic physical lags in fuel film flow from the nearly instantaneous air flow response. It demonstrates conclusively how the physical dimensions of the engine (specifically inlet pipe length) and the rotational engine speed directly alter the phase delay of maximum cylinder pressure during transients.65 This provides the physical baseline for the system's transport delay before digital controls were introduced.

### **Early Fuel Injection and Wall-Wetting Dynamics**

The transition from carburetors to Central Fuel Injection (CFI) and Port Fuel Injection (PFI) required explicit, parameterized modeling of fuel puddle dynamics. This puddle heavily influences the air-fuel ratio and, consequently, the residual torque generated during the initial phase of the settling tail.

**SAE 810494 (Aquino, 1981): *Transient A/F Control Characteristics of the 5 Liter Central Fuel Injection Engine*.** Aquino's paper is arguably the most referenced foundational dataset for transient engine dynamics regarding fuel films.65 Aquino conducted a highly controlled series of one-second throttle ramps (both tip-ins and tip-outs) on a 5.0L dynamometer engine.10 The resulting empirical data describes the manifold wall-wetting phenomena via a linear, first-order differential equation utilizing two critical parameters: a boiling time constant and a percent impaction parameter.10 The data demonstrates that the impaction parameter varies linearly with the throttle angle. The continuous-flow time-series graphs published in this paper are absolutely essential for researchers attempting to validate sub-exponential fuel decay models, as they map exactly how the fuel puddle evaporates and drives the engine during the deceleration phase.

### **Nonlinear and Adaptive Idle Speed Control**

As the 1980s progressed into the 1990s, control engineers realized that standard linearized engine models failed to accurately capture the true bifurcation dynamics of the idle region. Experiments shifted rapidly toward nonlinear control strategies, and the resulting publications contain explicit ![][image1] step-response plots.

**SAE 840443 (Mihelc & Citron, 1981): *An Adaptive Idle Speed Mode Control Design*.** This paper provides the foundational empirical data on applying early adaptive control algorithms to mitigate engine speed fluctuations and sub-harmonic vibrations at idle.59

**SAE 940974 (Kjergaard et al., 1994): *Advanced Nonlinear Engine Idle Speed Control Systems*.** This highly relevant paper compares the transient response of conventional PID and Linear Quadratic Regulator (LQR) designs against more advanced Input-Output Linearization and Sliding Mode controllers.62 The experimental data was gathered from an engine equipped with an early microprocessor-driven electronic throttle control. The plots within this paper provide direct visual evidence of the shape of the settling tail when the engine is subjected to sudden accessory load disturbances (e.g., power steering lock-up or air conditioning compressor engagement) forcing a rapid drop and recovery in RPM.62

**FISITA Proceedings 1996 (Hrovat): *MPC-based idle speed control for IC engine*.** Davor Hrovat's extensive, decades-long work at Ford Motor Company on Model Predictive Control (MPC) is pivotal to modern engine regulation. This paper provides experimental step-response data demonstrating how MPC utilizes an internal mathematical model of the plant to predict the future evolution of the system over a receding horizon.68 By optimizing the return-to-idle trajectory in real-time, the MPC effectively handles the intrinsic induction-to-power transport delays.59 The data from Hrovat's studies (often utilizing a 4.6L V8 engine) shows how predictive algorithms fundamentally alter the natural dissipative tail of the engine.70

**SAE 970027 (Yurkovich & Simpson, 1997): *Crank-angle domain modeling and control for idle speed*.** As noted in the Stanford repository section, the transition from time-domain to crank-angle domain sampling was critical for accurate high-resolution analysis. This paper highlights that transition, offering highly accurate, event-synchronized data on idle speed fluctuations and the mitigation of time-delay instabilities.67

## **Modern Open-Source Datasets for Retrospective Time-Series Analysis**

While historical university archives and SAE papers contain the targeted 1975–2000 contextual data, modern open-source repositories often host digitized, highly cleaned versions of similar test-bench telemetry. These modern datasets perfectly capture the driven-dissipative settling tail and can be immediately downloaded for statistical curve-fitting (e.g., in MATLAB or Python) to rigorously test for power-law versus exponential decay. The following datasets are explicitly named and readily accessible:

**1\. EngineFaultDB (Hosted on Mendeley Data / IEEE DataPort):** This is a remarkably comprehensive dataset containing 55,999 meticulously curated entries of automotive engine diagnostics. It includes 14 distinct variables sampled continuously as time-series data. The most critical variables for this research include Revolutions Per Minute (RPM), Manifold Absolute Pressure (MAP), Throttle Position Sensor (TPS), and Force (Torque measured in Newtons).72 By computationally searching the TPS and RPM columns sequentially, a researcher can isolate thousands of specific tip-out and rev-drop events. Extracting the RPM trace from the moment the TPS registers 0% down to the stabilized idle speed allows for massive statistical curve-fitting analysis to determine if the return-to-idle tail is exponentially or fractionally distributed across a vast sample size.

**2\. Electric Powertrain Jerk-Window Dataset (Hosted via arXiv/Experimental Repositories):** While this dataset involves electric machines rather than internal combustion engines, its structural relevance to transient torque control is immense. This dataset comprises 1,098 rear-axle jerk windows captured at a high resolution of 50 Hz.73 The tests were performed on an advanced drivetrain bench that enabled perfectly repeatable tip-in and tip-out torque steps across a vast operating range, from 207 to 14,886 RPM.73 Each data window contains 1.2 seconds of high-resolution transient data. Because an electric machine lacks the complex thermodynamic delays of an ICE (no wall-wetting, no induction-to-power delay), analyzing the non-stationary windows during negative torque commands (tip-outs) in this dataset provides definitive evidence of the *purely mechanical* damping profile of the rotating assembly. Comparing the exact curvature of this electric stabilization tail against an ICE stabilization tail will isolate the thermodynamic long-memory effects that cause the ICE to exhibit slower-than-exponential decay.

**3\. Motor Control Performance Dataset (Hosted on Kaggle):** This dataset contains 700 highly detailed simulated data points focusing on load disturbances, stabilization times, and Direct Torque Control (DTC).74 Key features include Time steps (s), Speed (RPM), Torque (Nm), and Stabilization Time (s) following an internal or external disturbance. Similar to the electric jerk dataset, the mathematical structure of the Proportional-Integral-Derivative (PID) optimization and the resulting time-series stabilization data perfectly mirrors the linear vs. nonlinear dissipative decay sought in engine transient research.74 Researchers can utilize this dataset to model how varying the PID integral gain artificially elongates the stabilization time, mimicking the electronic dashpot effect in an ICE.

**4\. Varying Speed Bearing Fault Dataset (Hosted on Mendeley Data):** Though originally intended for NVH (Noise, Vibration, and Harshness) analysis and fault detection, this dataset contains raw time-series vibration and highly accurate laser-based tachometer data for rotating assemblies decelerating and accelerating randomly between 680 RPM and 2460 RPM.75 Because the tachometer data is provided continuously as a raw time series, the deceleration curves—representing the total cessation of the driving force—can be extracted. This allows for the analysis of the natural mechanical friction and viscous dissipation of a heavy rotating assembly completely independent of combustion dynamics or ECU intervention.75

## **Synthesis and Concluding Remarks**

The internal combustion engine, when observed strictly through the lens of a return-to-idle transient, represents a textbook physical example of a complex driven-dissipative system abruptly transitioning from a highly active, heavily driven state into a stable limit cycle. For researchers seeking to definitively characterize the RPM settling tail—specifically to answer whether it adheres to a standard, memoryless exponential decay or a heavy-tailed, slower-than-exponential power law—the historical era spanning from 1975 to 2000 provides the most analytically transparent and rich data available. During this pivotal period, the mechanical simplicity of the base engine was paired for the first time with high-resolution digital telemetry and early microprocessors, allowing control researchers to rigorously attempt to isolate physical dissipation from advanced algorithmic interference.

To acquire the necessary time-series data to prove these mathematical hypotheses, researchers must bypass modern, highly synthesized textbook summaries and delve directly into the archival step-response plots and validation datasets housed within SAE Mobilus and specific university repositories. The foundational wall-wetting studies by Aquino (SAE 810494\) 10, the crank-angle synchronous event-based observer data generated by J.D. Powell's Guidance and Control Laboratory at Stanford 42, and the continuous-discrete Mean Value Engine Model (MVEM) multirate sampling data produced collaboratively by Ford Research and the University of Michigan 22 provide the exact ![][image1] time-series matrices required for extraction and digitization.

The empirical evidence buried within these archives, when viewed as a whole, strongly points to a definitive conclusion: while isolated theoretical mechanical models (such as the linear spring-dashpot Voigt element) predict a simple linear exponential decay, the holistic physical engine system exhibits a much more complex trajectory. Burdened by the delayed evaporation of port fuel films, the fast-scale equalization of manifold absolute pressure, the slow-scale shifting of thermal inertia and oil viscosity, and the artificial smoothing of ECU digital filter constants, the true return-to-idle transient exhibits a slower-than-exponential, asymptotic approach to the idle limit cycle.

This long-time, heavy-tailed decay is a hallmark of systems with multiple interacting relaxation rates and strong physical memory effects. By cross-referencing the historical SAE literature and university theses cataloged in this report with modern, digitized open-source telemetry—such as the EngineFaultDB 72 and electric powertrain jerk datasets 73—researchers possess the exact pointers necessary to mathematically validate the exact fractional decay rates and limit-cycle bifurcations of these ubiquitous driven-dissipative machines.
